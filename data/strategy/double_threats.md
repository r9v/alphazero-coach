# Double Threats and Trap Setups

A double threat (fork) exists when a player has two separate ways to complete four-in-a-row on their next move. Since the opponent can only block one threat per turn, a double threat guarantees a win. This is the primary winning mechanism in Connect 4.

## The Seven Trap (Figure-7)

The most well-known pattern in Connect 4. Three pieces form a diagonal line, and the top piece of the diagonal is also part of a three-piece horizontal line, creating a shape resembling the digit "7". This produces two completion points: one extending the diagonal and one extending the horizontal. The opponent can only block one. Most effective when built in or near the center of the board where both extending lines have room.

## The Open Three

Three pieces in a horizontal line with empty playable squares on both ends. The opponent blocks one end; the player completes the other. This only works if both end squares are actually playable (pieces below them exist) and neither end is blocked by the board edge or an opponent's piece.

## Stacked Threats

Multiple vertical threats in different columns simultaneously. The opponent can only respond to one column per turn. Especially effective when combined with horizontal or diagonal threats at the same height.

## Adjacent Threat Pairs

Two threats that share at least one piece (the pivot piece). Blocking one threat does not neutralize the shared piece that contributes to the other threat. Center-area pieces make the best pivots since they participate in more directions.

## Zig-Zag Patterns

Alternating pieces in a staircase pattern across columns creates multiple potential diagonal and horizontal lines. These patterns are harder to read and often lead to double threats.

## How to Construct Double Threats

1. Build threats that share a common pivot piece.
2. Use center-area pieces as pivot points since they participate in more directions.
3. Combine threats of different orientations (one horizontal + one diagonal) to make them harder to detect.
4. Build threats at different heights — having one threat on a lower row and another higher means the opponent must handle timing carefully.

## Defensive Awareness

- Block opponents at two-in-a-row before they reach three-in-a-row.
- Watch for diagonal setups that are less visible than horizontal ones.
- Pay attention to pieces that could become pivot points for future forks.
