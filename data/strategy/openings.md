# Opening Principles

Connect 4 was solved independently by James Dow Allen and Victor Allis in 1988. With perfect play, Player 1 wins by starting in the center column.

## First Move Analysis

- **Column 3 (center)**: The only winning first move. Accesses maximum winning lines and establishes central dominance.
- **Columns 2 or 4 (near-center)**: Reasonable but allows the opponent to equalize with perfect play. Still maintains some central influence.
- **Columns 1 or 5 (mid-board)**: Weak openings that surrender center and near-center to the opponent.
- **Columns 0 or 6 (edges)**: Worst possible openings. Starting on the edges allows the second player to force a win.

## Second Player Responses to Center Opening

- **Column 3 (on top of opponent)**: Contests the center directly. Most common expert response. Does not equalize — Player 1 retains advantage.
- **Columns 2 or 4 (adjacent to center)**: Claims important near-center territory. Reasonable but slightly less effective than a direct center contest.
- **Columns 1 or 5**: Weak response that concedes too much central territory.
- **Columns 0 or 6**: Worst possible response giving Player 1 an overwhelming positional advantage.

## Opening Principles

1. **Center first**: Always start center if you are Player 1.
2. **Contest the center**: If you are Player 2, play in or adjacent to the center column.
3. **Build low**: Establish pieces on the bottom rows first. Higher pieces need support beneath them.
4. **Maintain flexibility**: Do not commit to a single threat direction too early in the game.
5. **Avoid edges**: Edge columns in the opening waste tempo and surrender strategic territory.
6. **Think about parity**: Even in the opening, consider whether your threats will land on odd or even rows relative to your player number.

## Joseki

The term joseki (borrowed from Go) describes well-known opening sequences where both sides play optimally. After Player 1 opens in the center, the standard continuations have been extensively analyzed. Expert opening play focuses on establishing threats on rows with favorable parity while preventing the opponent from doing the same.
