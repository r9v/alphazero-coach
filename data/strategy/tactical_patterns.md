# Common Tactical Patterns

These are recurring patterns that arise in Connect 4 games. Recognizing them is key to both attacking and defending.

## The Seven Trap (Figure-7)

Three pieces in a diagonal plus the top diagonal piece is also part of a three-piece horizontal line, forming a "7" shape. Creates two completion points the opponent cannot both block. Most effective in the center of the board where both extending lines have room. Must be disrupted before completion — once formed it is typically game over.

## The Open Three (Horizontal Fork)

Three horizontal pieces with empty playable squares on both ends. The opponent blocks one end; the player completes the other. Only works if both end squares are playable (pieces below them exist) and neither end is blocked by the board edge or opponent pieces.

## Stacked Column Threat

Three pieces stacked vertically in one column. Forces the opponent to play on top of the stack — the only blocking move. The forced block may place the opponent's piece where it helps you elsewhere, or wastes their turn while you build threats in adjacent columns.

## The Diagonal Fork

A piece that simultaneously creates two diagonal threats (one ascending, one descending). Two diagonal completion points in different directions. Hard to construct but very hard for opponents to see coming.

## Low Threat Plus High Threat

One threat on a low row and another threat on a higher row in the same or adjacent column. Blocking the low threat places a piece that may enable the high threat, or vice versa. If both threats align with your row parity (odd for Player 1, even for Player 2), both are real threats that will eventually resolve in your favor.

## Tempo Chain

A sequence of threats where blocking one creates the next. You maintain the initiative for multiple consecutive turns, eventually ending with an unblockable double threat. Example: a horizontal threat forces a block; the blocking piece creates a vertical stack; the vertical block enables a diagonal.

## Bottom Trap

Pieces placed on the bottom row that set up multiple potential lines as the game progresses vertically. Early bottom-row pieces become the foundation for later horizontal and diagonal threats. Best executed with bottom-row pieces in the center columns to maximize future line participation.

## Column Pairing

Controlling pairs of adjacent columns (such as columns 2-3 or 3-4). Guarantees you can create horizontal threats while limiting the opponent's horizontal options. Counter this by establishing your own adjacent pair on the opposite side of the board.
