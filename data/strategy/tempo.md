# Tempo and Initiative

Tempo refers to which player is dictating the flow of the game — who is attacking versus who is reacting defensively.

## Forced Moves and Initiative

A forced move occurs when one player must block an immediate threat. The player who creates forced moves holds the initiative because they dictate where the opponent plays. Chains of forced moves allow a player to control the board for multiple turns in succession.

## Tempo Chains

A tempo chain is a sequence of threats where blocking one creates the next. For example: a horizontal threat forces a block; the blocking piece creates a vertical stack; blocking the vertical enables a diagonal. The attacker maintains initiative for multiple consecutive turns, eventually ending with an unblockable double threat.

## Wasting Moves Strategically

Sometimes the best move does not create an immediate threat but instead forces the opponent to waste their turn. Playing a piece in a non-critical column can force the opponent to respond elsewhere while preserving your own threat structure. This is analogous to a "quiet move" in chess — it does not threaten immediately but improves your position.

## Zugzwang as Tempo Control

In Connect 4 endgames, being forced to move can be a disadvantage because placing a piece beneath your opponent's threat activates that threat. Controlling zugzwang means engineering the board state so your opponent is the one forced to make the losing move. Victor Allis's VICTOR program was specifically designed to always retain control of the zugzwang.

## Tempo in the Opening

The first player inherently has the initiative. They should maintain tempo by always having at least one active threat. If the first player loses tempo by playing a purely defensive move with no threat component, the second player can seize the initiative.

## Practical Tempo Guidelines

- Always look for moves that both defend AND create a threat.
- Avoid purely reactive moves when possible — even blocking moves should advance your own position.
- Count the number of free turns you have before you must respond to opponent threats.
- Plan threats in sequence so that blocking one threat sets up the next.
