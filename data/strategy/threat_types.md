# Threat Types: Vertical, Horizontal, and Diagonal

Different threat orientations have different properties. Understanding these differences is key to both attacking and defending.

## Horizontal Threats

The most visible threat type — easiest for both players to see and block. The opponent simply places a piece in the missing column within the row. Horizontal threats through the center columns are strongest because they have more room to extend in either direction. Weak alone but powerful when combined with other threat types to form double threats.

## Vertical Threats

Stacking three pieces in a column creates a vertical threat where the fourth space is directly above. Vertical threats have a unique property: there is only one way to block them — place a piece directly on top. This makes vertical threats "single-response," meaning they cannot form a double threat by themselves.

However, vertical threats are excellent for controlling WHERE the opponent plays. Forcing an opponent to block a vertical threat in one column may allow you to play a winning move in another column. Once three pieces are stacked, the only defense is playing on top, which may not be strategically desirable for the opponent.

## Diagonal Threats

The hardest threat type for human players to spot, often overlooked until too late. Building diagonals requires pieces on adjacent columns at incrementally different rows. The gravity constraint makes diagonals the hardest to build intentionally because you need supporting pieces below each diagonal cell.

Diagonal threats are considered the most potent because opponents frequently miss them, they naturally create pivot points for double threats, and they interact with both horizontal and vertical lines through their component pieces.

## Comparative Strategic Value

1. Diagonal threats are hardest to detect and best for creating forks.
2. Vertical threats offer the most board control through forced responses.
3. Horizontal threats are easiest to complete but also easiest to block.
4. The strongest play combines multiple threat types — a piece that threatens both a diagonal and a horizontal completion is extremely powerful.
5. The Seven Trap combines a diagonal threat with a horizontal threat, which is why it is one of the most effective patterns in the game.

## How Threat Types Interact

A piece in the center column can simultaneously participate in vertical, horizontal, and both diagonal directions. Building threats at different heights across different orientations makes defense extremely difficult. The best double threats combine two different orientations because the opponent must think in multiple directions simultaneously.
